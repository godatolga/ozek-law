🚨
An expungement or sealed record under state law does NOT eliminate the conviction for immigration purposes. USCIS requires disclosure of ALL arrests, charges, and convictions — including dismissed cases and those you completed community service for. Failure to disclose is misrepresentation, which is itself a permanent ground of inadmissibility. Bring certified court dispositions for everything.

Legal Requirements — Know Your Case

The USCIS officer will evaluate each element below. Know how you satisfy each one before walking into the interview room.

⚖️ INA 245(a) — General Adjustment of Status Requirements
1
Inspected & Admitted or Paroled
You must have been lawfully admitted or paroled into the United States. Entry without inspection (EWI) generally bars adjustment under 245(a).
2
Maintained Lawful Status
You must have maintained continuous lawful status since your entry, with limited exceptions under INA 245(k) for employment-based applicants (up to 180 aggregate days of violation).
3
Eligible for Immigrant Visa
An immigrant visa must be immediately available to you. Your priority date must be current in the Visa Bulletin at the time of filing and at interview.
4
Approved I-140 Petition
Your I-140 petition must be approved or approvable. For self-petitioners, the petition must remain valid.
5
Not Subject to Bars
You must not be subject to grounds of inadmissibility under INA 212(a) that have not been waived. This includes criminal, health-related, security, fraud, and public charge grounds.
📅
The 245(k) Exception
If you are an employment-based applicant and had periods out of status, unauthorized work, or status violations, you may still adjust if the aggregate total of those violations does not exceed 180 days. This exception only applies if the violations occurred AFTER your last lawful admission. Know your history — your attorney should have analyzed this.
🔄
AC21 Portability
If your I-485 has been pending 180 days or more, you may change employers or job positions under INA 204(j) (AC21 portability), as long as the new job is in the same or similar occupational classification. Your attorney must have a portability letter on file. Know whether this applies to you.
📋
Study Your Application
Re-read your entire I-485, your I-140, and the supporting documents before the interview. The officer's questions come directly from your application. Inconsistent answers — even unintentional — raise red flags.
🚫
Bars to Adjustment
Certain grounds of inadmissibility are permanent bars — including willful misrepresentation, fraud in a prior immigration proceeding, a prior removal order without a waiver, aggravated felony convictions, and certain controlled substance offenses. If any of these apply, discuss with your attorney before the interview.

⚖️ You Will Be Under Oath — What That Means

The USCIS officer will place you under oath before asking a single question. Everything you say from that moment is sworn testimony. This has serious legal consequences.

🚨
Lying under oath to a federal officer is a federal crime under 18 U.S.C. 1001 and 18 U.S.C. 1621. It is also an independent ground of inadmissibility under INA 212(a)(6)(C). A single false statement can result in a permanent bar to all immigration benefits — regardless of the underlying merits of your petition. If you believe a question may touch on a sensitive issue in your history, discuss it with your attorney before the interview, not during it.
💬
If You Are Unsure of an Answer
It is always acceptable to say "I do not remember" or "I would need to check my records." The officer may ask for your best estimate — at which point clarify that you are not certain. An honest uncertainty is far better than a confident incorrect answer.
🔍
Consistency Is Critical
Your oral answers must be consistent with what is in your written application. USCIS officers have your complete file on screen. If you say something different from your application, the officer will note it and it may trigger an RFE, a NOID, or denial.
📄
If Your Application Has an Error
Do NOT lie at the interview to match a mistake in your application. The interview is the correct time to correct an error. Tell the officer: "I need to correct an answer on my application." Your attorney should be notified of any errors before the interview date.
☎️
After the Interview
Write down everything immediately after leaving. If the officer issues an RFE, NOID, or requests additional evidence, contact Ozek Law Firm the same day. Do not respond to any USCIS correspondence without your attorney.

Interview Tips & Strategy

From the desk of Tolga Ozek, Esq. — what to know before you walk into the USCIS interview room.

⚠️
Important: Always consult with Ozek Law Firm before your interview. If your attorney will not be present, confirm this arrangement in advance. Call +1 (202) 854-8545 with any questions.
🤝
Understand Your Attorney's Role
Your attorney cannot answer questions for you. The officer will direct all questions to you. Your attorney is there to observe, correct miscommunications, and raise legal objections — not to coach you or signal answers. Do not look to your attorney for cues during the interview.
🗣️
Answer Only What Is Asked
Do not volunteer information that was not requested. Keep your answers concise. If a question can be answered with yes or no, answer with yes or no. Long unprompted answers create additional questions and extend the interview — sometimes into territory that causes problems.
📋
Know Your Application Cold
Review your I-485, I-140, and all supporting documents before the interview. The officer is reading from your file in real time. Know what your application says — especially dates, addresses, employer names, and job titles. Inconsistencies between oral answers and the written record are the most common source of problems.
🚫
Never Lie to a Federal Officer
Misrepresentation to USCIS is a permanent ground of inadmissibility under INA 212(a)(6)(C) and a federal crime. If a sensitive question is anticipated — criminal record, status gap, prior unauthorized work — discuss the answer with your attorney before the interview. There is no such thing as a "small" lie in an immigration interview.
⏰
Arrive Early — Know Where You Are Going
Arrive 15-20 minutes before your appointment time. Know the exact USCIS office address. Know whether your attorney is meeting you there or attending by phone. Being late can result in your interview being rescheduled — a delay of months. Bring a government-issued photo ID for security screening.
👔
Dress Professionally
Dress in business casual or better. Do not wear jeans, athletic clothing, t-shirts, or casual footwear. Your appearance sends a message about how seriously you are taking the process. Dress as you would for a professional job interview.
📁
Organize Your Documents
Organize your documents in a tabbed folder or binder in the same order as the checklist in this guide. If the officer asks for a specific document, you should be able to find it within seconds. Fumbling through papers creates a poor impression and can raise questions about whether your documents are genuine.
✍️
Write Everything Down Immediately After
The moment you leave the interview room, write down every question that was asked and every answer you gave — especially anything that surprised you. If the officer requested additional documents or expressed any concern, note it precisely. Contact Ozek Law Firm the same day: +1 (202) 854-8545.
What to Expect at the USCIS Office
1
Security Screening
Government photo ID required. Electronics and large bags may be restricted. Arrive early to allow for security.
2
Check In & Wait
Present your appointment notice at the front desk. Wait in the waiting area. Officers may observe the waiting room.
3
The Oath
You will be sworn in before any questions are asked. Everything you say is sworn testimony from this point forward.
4
The Interview
Typically 20-60 minutes for employment-based cases. The officer reads from your file and asks questions from it.
5
Decision or Pending
Case may be approved on the spot, sent for further processing, or trigger an RFE. An RFE is NOT a denial.
